About the North Atlantic Right Whale Consortium ▼

Started in 1986 as a collaborative data sharing group, the North Atlantic Right Whale Consortium (NARWC) has grown to include more than 200 organizations and individuals representing research and conservation organizations, shipping and fishing industries, technical experts, U.S. and Canadian government agencies, and state and provincial authorities, all of whom are dedicated to the conservation and recovery of the North Atlantic right whale. North Atlantic Right Whale Consortium members agreed in 2004 that an annual "report card" on the status of right whales would be useful. This report card includes updates on the status of the cataloged population, mortalities and injury events, and a summary of management and research efforts that have occurred over the previous 12 months.

05 · Management

Conservation & Management Actions

Text
NOAA Fisheries is cautiously optimistic: species abundance is increasing and the sharp rise in mortalities over the last decade appears to have slowed, though human-caused mortalities and serious injuries continue.
The North Atlantic Right Whale Road to Recovery framework describes ongoing NOAA efforts across the agency, built on the Endangered Species Act and Marine Mammal Protection Act, complementing the 2021–2025 Priority Action Plan.
NOAA continues to focus on technological solutions to reduce vessel strikes and entanglements, promoting sustainable coexistence between marine industries and right whales.
Canada's Budget 2023 provided $151.9 million over three years across DFO, Transport Canada, Environment and Climate Change Canada, and Parks Canada to continue protecting endangered whales.
Since 2018, DFO has enacted dynamic fishing closures in the Gulf of St. Lawrence, Bay of Fundy, and Roseway Basin — covering over 60,256 km² of temporary and season-long closures in 2025.
New for 2025: updated Shallow Water Protocol with revised and additional depth management lines (5, 10, 15, 20 fathoms) for more targeted closures reflecting actual bathymetry.
Whalesafe Gear Pilots launched in LFAs 36, 38 and shallow water areas: required harvesters to reduce vertical buoy lines by >50% and use low breaking-strength (LBS) gear modifications.
DFO Science aerial surveillance accumulated 602 hours 37 minutes of flight time; Conservation & Protection branch conducted 334 flights totaling 758 hours 45 minutes in 2025 season.
Ghost Gear Program: since 2020 has retrieved more than 43,300 units of gear, over 2,540 tonnes of marine debris, and 970 km of rope from Atlantic, Arctic, and Pacific coasts.
In February 2025, DFO hosted the 2nd International Fishing Gear Innovation Summit in Moncton, bringing together global experts including Indigenous and non-Indigenous harvesters.
On November 27, 2025, DFO published an updated Recovery Potential Assessment for the North Atlantic Right Whale to support development of an amended recovery strategy.
Transport Canada implemented a mandatory static speed restriction zone covering much of the Gulf of St. Lawrence (65,000+ km²), plus dynamic speed restriction zones — applying to all vessels longer than 13 m from April 16 to November 15, 2025.
A mandatory restricted area of 4,000 km² around the Shediac Valley was in effect June 16–September 26, 2025, closed to all vessels >13 m with speeds limited to 8 knots.
Seasonal Management Areas temporarily expanded the static speed restriction zone April 16–June 24, 2025, during peak whale transit period into the Gulf of St. Lawrence.
In 2025, radio communications were significantly expanded to include shipping lanes for direct Canadian Coast Guard vessel slowdown requests when whales are detected.
Three acoustic underwater gliders were deployed in dynamic shipping zones to detect right whales, complementing aerial surveillance from the National Aerial Surveillance Program (NASP).
Voluntary seasonal slowdown in Cabot Strait continued; Fall 2025 period was extended by three weeks (September 3 to November 15).
